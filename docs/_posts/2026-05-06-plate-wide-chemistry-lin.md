**Plate-wide Chemistry: From Hits to Evidence**

Most discovery workflows treat molecules as isolated decisions: design one compound, make it, test it, and iterate. This approach is precise, but it makes early signals fragile. A single hit is difficult to interpret, especially in phenotypic systems where mechanism is unclear.

Plate-wide chemistry changes the structure of this process. Instead of optimizing molecules, it expands them. Starting from weak hits or fragments, it generates many compounds in parallel, allowing multiple directions in chemical space to be tested at once.

This does not produce clean structure–activity relationships. The resulting molecules are often substantially different, and activity does not vary smoothly. What matters is not continuity, but recurrence — whether activity appears repeatedly across independent expansions.

Plate-wide chemistry does not validate individual molecules. It tests whether a signal survives expansion. A single active compound is ambiguous. Multiple active compounds derived from the same starting point form evidence.

This makes it particularly valuable early in discovery, where the goal is not yet to optimize a molecule, but to decide which directions are worth pursuing.

Plate-wide chemistry is not about making many compounds.  
It is about turning isolated hits into structured evidence.
