**Organ-on-Chip: Biology as a Living Interface**

Most discovery experiments work by separating biological effects so they can be measured independently. This strategy is extremely effective, but it also removes many of the interfaces through which biology actually operates.

Organ-on-chip systems sit between plate assays and mouse models. They do not recreate organisms. They selectively restore structure — gradients, barriers, and communication between tissues — while keeping experiments interpretable.

This makes them especially useful when isolating components removes the phenomenon being studied, which is often the case in oncology, neuroscience, and metabolic disease.

Rather than increasing complexity uniformly, organ-on-chip experiments rebuild just enough biology to test where experimental simplification begins to fail.

Biology is not only molecular. It is relational.
