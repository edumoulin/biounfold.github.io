**Short version — Toxicity: When Discovery Meets Consequence**

*Drug discovery is built on reductionism.*

We isolate proteins, study pathways individually, and optimize molecules against specific targets. This approach works remarkably well. It allows scientists to understand mechanisms and design increasingly selective drugs.

But toxicity appears when the molecule leaves these controlled systems and enters the full organism.

Unlike potency or exposure, toxicity is not a single property. It is the consequence of a molecule interacting with biology across tissues, time, and physiological contexts.

Many toxic effects are explained by off-target binding. Even weak interactions can become relevant when exposure is high or sustained, which is why selectivity profiling has become a central part of modern discovery.

Yet binding alone does not explain everything.

Biological systems are highly interconnected. A drug can bind its intended target with high specificity and still produce adverse effects because the pathway it perturbs is linked to other essential processes. In these cases toxicity emerges from on-target effects occurring in the wrong biological context.
