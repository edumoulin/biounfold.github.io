Most biotech companies do not bring their own drugs to patients.

They discover them — and then license them.

Modern drug discovery runs on two different execution engines.

Discovery organizations optimize exploration:
rapid experiments, evolving hypotheses, directional biological signals.

Clinical development organizations optimize consistency:
standardized trials, regulatory alignment, large patient populations, multi-year execution.

No single organization can optimize both at the same time.

Licensing connects these systems.

It also solves a deeper problem: unfinished drugs do not yet have a measurable value.

At the time of a licensing deal:
• the molecule may not have entered late clinical trials
• it may not even have been tested in humans
• only ~5–10% of programs ultimately succeed

Instead of assigning a single price, value is distributed across time:
upfront payments price credibility
milestones distribute uncertainty
royalties preserve exposure to success

Biotech companies do not monetize drugs.

They monetize credible progress toward drugs.

Licensing is the mechanism that allows discovery and development to function as a coordinated system.
